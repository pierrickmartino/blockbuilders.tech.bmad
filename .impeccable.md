# Impeccable Design Context — Blockbuilders

This file is the canonical source of design context for the Blockbuilders project. It is consumed by Impeccable skills (and any other design tooling) to ensure every interface decision is grounded in who the product is for, how it should feel, and what it must never look like.

Keep this file up to date. If brand voice, audience, or aesthetic direction shifts, edit the relevant section here first — skills read from this file, not from tribal knowledge.

---

## Design Context

### Users

**Who:** Retail crypto traders — individuals, not institutions. They are serious about building an edge but are self-directed, not professional desk traders. They use the product on a laptop during focused sessions, not glanced at on a phone between tasks.

**Context of use:** A deliberate, seated session. The trader is building, iterating, or reviewing a strategy. They are not scrolling for entertainment and they are not in a 30-second push-to-trade flow. Sessions are minutes-to-hours long and demand sustained attention.

**Job-to-be-done:** Build, backtest, and iterate on crypto trading strategies visually — without writing code — on major pairs (BTC/USDT, ETH/USDT). Turn a hunch into a validated system. The product replaces "open Python and a notebook" for users who don't want to code but want more rigor than eyeballing a chart.

**What they are NOT:** They are not gamified retail day-traders chasing dopamine, not beginners learning what a candle is, and not institutional quants who'd prefer an SDK. Designing toward any of those three audiences is a miss.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** **Disciplined · Precise · Confident.**

**Voice:** Engineered, unhurried, matter-of-fact. Reads like a well-written instrument panel — no sales language, no exclamation points, no motivational copy. Uses mono for numbers because numbers deserve it, not because it looks "technical."

**Emotional goal mid-session:** *Confident and in-control.* When a trader is placing blocks or watching a backtest run, the interface should make their moves feel backed by a serious engine — key elements carry a small amount of weight and presence, the rest stays quiet. Do NOT aim for anxious-cockpit intensity or spa-like calm. Aim for "a good workstation."

**Tone in microcopy:**
- Correct: "Backtest complete. 142 trades over 18 months."
- Wrong (too casual): "All done! Your strategy ran 142 trades 🎉"
- Wrong (too cold): "OPERATION TERMINATED. N=142."

### Aesthetic Direction

**Theme name:** *Signal* — already committed, codified in `frontend/src/app/globals.css` and `docs/design/design-system.pen`. This Design Context should be read as *what Signal means*, not a new direction.

**Core principle:** **Signal over decoration.** Every pixel earns its place by conveying state, hierarchy, or meaning. Decoration that doesn't carry information is cut.

**Visual vocabulary:**
- Flat fills, thin 1px rules, restrained two-layer shadows
- Generous quiet space around dense information blocks (64px section gaps are intentional)
- Color is semantic (success/error/warning) or categorical (canvas node types) — never mood-setting
- Dense-but-ordered: information-rich views that still breathe, achieved through rhythm not sparsity
- Left-aligned composition, asymmetric where it serves hierarchy
- Numbers and data in mono (IBM Plex Mono default); UI labels in sans (IBM Plex Sans default)

**Palette** (from `globals.css`, Signal theme):
- Primary blue: `hsl(204 65% 57%)` light / `hsl(204 76% 63%)` dark — reserved for key actions and info
- Semantic: green (success), red (error), amber (warning)
- Canvas categories: purple (input), blue (indicator), amber (logic), green (signal), red (risk)
- Neutrals tint slightly toward the primary hue — maintain this when introducing new surfaces
- Both light and dark modes are first-class; dark uses `#0C0C0E` background, not pure black

**Typography:** IBM Plex Sans + IBM Plex Mono are the current defaults and treated as part of the identity, but the pairing is *open to reconsideration* if a more distinctive pair serves the Signal brief better. Any proposed alternative must:
1. Still pair a sans or neo-grotesque with a mono for numbers
2. Feel institutional/engineered, not "designy" or editorial
3. Read well at small sizes in dense data tables
If you propose a change, surface it as a deliberate recommendation — don't swap silently.

**References (what TO feel like):**
- A disciplined trading workstation, not a landing page
- Editorial restraint of a technical manual or an exhibit caption
- Instruments that professionals choose because they trust them

**Anti-references (what NOT to feel like):**
- **Crypto/Web3 maximalism** — neon-on-dark, holographic gradients, glowing orbs, cyberpunk accents, animated tickers for mood. This is the single most important thing Blockbuilders must not look like.
- Gamified retail broker apps (Robinhood-style confetti, oversized green/red, push-to-trade excitement)
- Playful no-code tools (pastel, rounded, mascots, Zapier/Make aesthetic)
- Generic purple-gradient SaaS dashboards and sparkline-as-decoration
- "AI slop" tells from Impeccable's DON'T list: colored side-stripe borders, gradient text, glassmorphism, identical card grids, rounded-icon-above-heading templates

### Design Principles

These are the tiebreakers. When two options look equally reasonable, pick the one that better satisfies the higher principle.

1. **Signal over decoration.** If an element doesn't carry state, hierarchy, or meaning, cut it. A quieter interface is almost always the right call.
2. **Earn the reader's trust through restraint.** No hype language, no decorative gradients, no motion for motion's sake. Seriousness is the product.
3. **Numbers are first-class citizens.** Financial data gets typographic respect — tabular figures, mono weight, aligned decimals, semantic color only when it changes meaning.
4. **Density with rhythm, not density with noise.** Information-dense views are correct for this audience — but vary spacing and weight so the eye has somewhere to rest. Avoid monotonous identical grids.
5. **Light and dark are equals.** Both themes are shipped. Neither is the "safe" default. Design every component for both from the start — don't retrofit.
6. **Intentional color.** Primary blue for key actions and info. Semantic colors only for state. Category colors only on canvas nodes. A new accent needs a reason that isn't "it looked nice."
